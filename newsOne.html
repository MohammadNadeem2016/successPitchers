Zoom has joined tech industry counterterrorism group

Zoom, a video-conferencing platform, has joined a counter-terrorism group led by Meta Platforms Inc (FB.O), formerly known as Facebook, and Microsoft Corp (MSFT.O), according to the group's announcement on Wednesday.

The Global Internet Forum to Counter Terrorism (GIFCT) is a non-profit organisation whose members share information about how to resist terrorism and violent extremism on their websites.

Zoom Video Communications Inc (ZM.O), whose platform enjoyed massive growth during the COVID-19 outbreak but has slowed in recent months, has been under fire for how it polices content and abuses.

Facebook, Microsoft, Twitter (TWTR.N), and Alphabet's (GOOGL.O) YouTube were among the GIFCT's original members.

It now has 18 members, including home-rental company Airbnb (ABNB.O), social network Tumblr, and online publishing platform WordPress, which all joined this year. It stated that more will be added in 2022.

Tech corporations have often been chastised for failing to prevent violent extremism on their platforms, but there are also worries about free speech.

Following a succession of fatal incidents in Paris and Brussels, the GIFCT was formed in 2017 under pressure from the US and European governments.

It runs a database where member firms may share "hashes," which are unique numerical representations of original material that has been deleted from their services. These hashes can be used by other organisations to detect similar content on other sites and evaluate or delete it.

In a statement, Josh Parecki, Zoom's assistant general counsel for trust and safety, stated, "It is our job to help our users and safeguard them from online dangers."

"We want to make the digital world a safer place for everybody by cooperating with other industry leaders, sharing crucial learnings, and advancing research."

Zoom encountered high-profile content filtering concerns when consumers and businesses turned to video-conferencing applications under COVID-19 lockout limitations.

These ranged from abuses such as "zoombombing," in which uninvited users crashed others' calls to share hate speech, racist, violent, or pornographic content, to decisions such as the cancellation of a virtual event featuring Leila Khaled, a member of a Palestinian group on the US State Department's terrorist list.
